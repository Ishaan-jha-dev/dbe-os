# Module 1: Forces of Demand and Supply

## 1.1 Welcome to the Module
### Overview of Microeconomics
#### Core Objective
The primary goal of this module is to introduce the fundamental principles governing individual decision-making by consumers and firms. It establishes a framework for understanding how resources are allocated through the forces of demand and supply.

#### Three Major Course Objectives
*   Learning economic concepts for better decision-making.
*   Understanding real-world problems through an economic lens.
*   Mastering the glossary and language of economics.

---

## 1.2 Introduction to Microeconomics
### Defining Economics
#### Scarcity and Choice
Economics is the study of how individuals, businesses, and societies make choices to allocate **scarce resources** efficiently to satisfy unlimited wants.

### Branches of Economics
| Feature | Microeconomics | Macroeconomics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Individual units (households, firms). | The economy as a whole (nations, governments). |
| **Key Decisions** | How much to save/consume; how a firm prices a product. | Measuring national income, growth trajectories, inflation. |
| **Trade-offs** | Allocation of limited inputs to product lines. | Welfare schemes vs. industrial subsidies. |

### Economic Methodology
#### The Use of Models
*   **Definition:** Models are mathematical representations of variables or "maps" of the real world.
*   **Purpose:** They simplify reality by omitting unimportant variables to focus on major forces.
*   **Function:** Models bring discipline to thought. As George Box stated: "All models are wrong, but some are useful."

#### Equity versus Efficiency Trade-off
*   **Efficiency:** Relates to the size of the "economic pie" (obtaining maximum output from scarce resources).
*   **Equity:** Relates to how the "pie" is divided (the fairness of distribution of benefits among members of society).
*   **The Conflict:** Policies designed to increase equity (like taxing the rich to fund welfare) can reduce efficiency by diminishing the incentive to work and produce.

---

## 1.3 Conceptual Tools of an Economist
### 1.3.1 Opportunity Cost
#### Definition
The **Opportunity Cost** of a decision is the value of the next best alternative that must be given up to pursue that decision.

*   **Implicit Nature:** These costs are often not visible (implicit) and are frequently ignored in decision-making.
*   **Subjectivity:** It varies per individual (e.g., for some, the cost of a BBA is lost wages; for others, it is lost leisure time).
*   **Decision Rule:** To think like an economist, one must never ignore the opportunity cost. If opportunity costs are missed, benefits are overstated.

### 1.3.2 Sunk Cost
#### Definition
**Sunk Costs** are costs that have already been incurred and cannot be recovered or altered by any future decision.

*   **Sunk Cost Fallacy:** The mistake of letting past unrecoverable costs influence current decisions (e.g., staying in a bad movie just because the ticket was expensive).
*   **Decision Rule:** Always ignore sunk costs when making a cost-benefit analysis. Decisions should be based on future costs and benefits only.

### 1.3.3 The Marginal Principle
#### Definition
The **Marginal Principle** is a heuristic for benefit maximization. It suggests comparing the additional (marginal) benefits of an activity to its additional (marginal) costs.

*   **Marginal Benefit ($MB$):** The change in total benefit resulting from one additional unit of activity.
*   **Marginal Cost ($MC$):** The change in total cost resulting from one additional unit of activity.
*   **Net Benefit:** $Total Benefit - Total Cost$.

#### The Decision Rule
*   If $MB > MC$: Increase the activity to increase net benefit.
*   If $MB < MC$: Decrease the activity.
*   **Optimization Point:** Net benefit is maximized when $MB = MC$.

---

## 1.4 Law of Demand
### 1.4.1 Fundamentals of Demand
#### Definition of Quantity Demanded
The amount of a good that buyers are **willing and able** to purchase at a specific price. Demand requires both the desire for the good and the financial capacity to afford it.

#### The Law of Demand
As the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases, holding all other factors constant (**Ceteris Paribus**). This results in a **downward-sloping** demand curve.

### 1.4.2 Demand Schedule and Curve
*   **Demand Schedule:** A table showing the relationship between price and quantity demanded.
*   **Demand Curve:** A graphical representation of the demand schedule, plotting price on the $y$-axis and quantity on the $x$-axis.
*   **Market Demand:** The sum of all individual quantities demanded at every given price.

### 1.4.3 Determinants of Demand (Shifts vs. Movements)
*   **Movement along the curve:** Caused solely by a change in the **Price** of the good itself.
*   **Shift of the curve:** Caused by factors other than price.

| Factor | Effect on Demand |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Income (Normal Good)** | Increase in income $\rightarrow$ Shift Right (Increase). |
| **Income (Inferior Good)** | Increase in income $\rightarrow$ Shift Left (Decrease). |
| **Price of Substitutes** | Price of substitute $A$ rises $\rightarrow$ Demand for good $B$ rises (Shift Right). |
| **Price of Complements** | Price of complement $A$ rises $\rightarrow$ Demand for good $B$ falls (Shift Left). |
| **Preferences/Tastes** | Favorable change (e.g., celebrity influence) $\rightarrow$ Shift Right. |
| **Expectations** | Expect higher future prices $\rightarrow$ Current demand rises (Shift Right). |
| **Number of Buyers** | More buyers $\rightarrow$ Shift Right. |

---

## 1.5 Law of Supply
### 1.5.1 Fundamentals of Supply
#### Definition of Quantity Supplied
The amount of a good that sellers are **willing and able** to sell at a given price.

#### The Law of Supply
As the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases, holding other factors constant. This results in an **upward-sloping** supply curve.
*   **Intuition:** Higher prices make it profitable to use less productive resources (e.g., a farmer using less fertile land because the high price covers the higher production cost).

### 1.5.2 Supply Schedule and Curve
*   **Supply Schedule:** A table showing price and quantity supplied.
*   **Supply Curve:** Graphical representation; upward sloping.
*   **Market Supply:** The sum of the quantities supplied by all sellers at each price.

### 1.5.3 Determinants of Supply (Shifts vs. Movements)
*   **Movement along the curve:** Caused only by a change in the **Price** of the good.
*   **Shift of the curve:** Caused by changes in production conditions.

| Factor | Effect on Supply |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Input Prices** | Increase in cost of inputs (labor, raw materials) $\rightarrow$ Shift Left (Decrease). |
| **Technology** | Improvement in technology $\rightarrow$ Shift Right (Increase). |
| **Expectations** | Expect higher future prices $\rightarrow$ Current supply falls (hoarding) $\rightarrow$ Shift Left. |
| **Number of Sellers** | Increase in sellers $\rightarrow$ Shift Right. |

---

## 1.6 Practice Problems
### 1.6.1 Supply Function Calculation
Given a supply function for HDTVs ($Q_{sx}$):
$$Q_{sx} = 2000 + 3P_x - 4P_y - P_i$$
Where:
*   $P_x$ = Price of HDTVs
*   $P_y$ = Price of tablets
*   $P_i$ = Price of inputs

**Scenario:** $P_x = 14,000$, $P_y = 10,000$, $P_i = 2,000$.
**Calculation:**
$$Q_{sx} = 2000 + 3(14,000) - 4(10,000) - 2000$$
$$Q_{sx} = 2000 + 42,000 - 40,000 - 2000 = 2000 \text{ units.}$$

### 1.6.2 Demand Function Calculation
Given a demand function for Good X ($Q_x$):
$$Q_x = 10,200 - 3P_x + 4P_y - I + 0.02A_x$$
Where:
*   $I$ = Income, $A_x$ = Advertising.
*   **Substitute vs. Complement:** Since the coefficient of $P_y$ is positive ($+4$), X and Y are **Substitutes**.
*   **Normal vs. Inferior:** If income increases and $Q_x$ decreases (negative coefficient for $I$), the good is **Normal** (per transcript logic regarding the study of tradeoffs).

## 1.8 Key Takeaways
### Market Types
1.  **Perfectly Competitive:** Many small buyers and sellers; identical goods; no single participant influences price.
2.  **Oligopoly:** A few large sellers (e.g., satellite launches, insulin market).
3.  **Monopoly:** Only one single seller (e.g., specific patented medicines).

## Quick Revision Summary
*   **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative given up.
*   **Sunk Cost:** Costs that cannot be recovered; ignore them in decision-making.
*   **Marginal Principle:** Continue an activity as long as $MB \ge MC$.
*   **Law of Demand:** Price $\uparrow$, Quantity Demanded $\downarrow$ (Inverse relationship).
*   **Law of Supply:** Price $\uparrow$, Quantity Supplied $\uparrow$ (Positive relationship).
*   **Shift:** Change in factors other than price.
*   **Movement:** Change in the price of the good itself.


# Module 2: Equilibrium in a Competitive Market

## 2.1 Welcome to the Module
### Overview
This module explores how market forces interact to determine prices and quantities. It covers:
*   **Market Equilibrium:** The balance between supply and demand.
*   **Surplus Analysis:** Measuring economic welfare for buyers and sellers.
*   **Elasticity:** Quantifying responsiveness to price and income changes.
*   **Government Intervention:** The impact of taxes, price ceilings, and price floors.

---

## 2.2 Market Equilibrium
### 2.2.1 Defining Equilibrium
#### Equilibrium Price ($P^*$) and Quantity ($Q^*$)
Market equilibrium occurs at the single point where the **demand curve** and **supply curve** intersect.
*   **Market Clearing:** At $P^*$, the quantity demanded by buyers exactly equals the quantity supplied by sellers ($QD = QS$).
*   **Stability:** If the price is moved away from equilibrium, market forces (self-interested behavior) push it back.

### 2.2.2 Disequilibrium: Surplus and Shortage
| Condition | Market State | Symptom | Pressure on Price |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $P > P^*$ | **Excess Supply (Surplus)** | Rising inventories; infrequent consumers. | **Downward** (Sellers offer discounts). |
| $P < P^*$ | **Excess Demand (Shortage)** | Vanishing inventory; long queues/waiting times. | **Upward** (Buyers bid higher prices). |

### 2.2.3 The Invisible Hand of Prices
#### Coordination without Central Authority
Prices act as signals that coordinate millions of independent decisions.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Prices determine who gets a scarce resource (those willing to pay) and who produces it (those with costs below the price).
*   **Rationing:** Prices adjust until the market clears, ensuring efficient distribution without a central planner.

### 2.2.4 Analyzing Shifts in Equilibrium
To predict price trends after a news event, follow this **Four-Step Checklist**:
1.  **Identify the Market:** Determine the specific product being studied.
2.  **Determine the Shift:** Decide if the event shifts the demand curve, the supply curve, or both.
3.  **Identify the Direction:** Does the curve shift right (increase) or left (decrease)?
4.  **Find the New Equilibrium:** Use the D&S diagram to see how $P^*$ and $Q^*$ change.

---

## 2.3 Consumer and Producer Surplus
### 2.3.1 Consumer Surplus (CS)
#### Definition
The benefit buyers receive from participating in a market.
*   **Willingness to Pay (WTP):** The maximum amount a buyer is willing to pay for a good; represents the monetary value of their utility.
*   **Formula:** $$CS = \text{Willingness to Pay} - \text{Price Paid}$$
*   **Graphical Representation:** The area **below the demand curve** and **above the price**.

### 2.3.2 Producer Surplus (PS)
#### Definition
The benefit sellers receive from participating in a market.
*   **Cost:** The lowest price a seller is willing to accept (includes raw materials and opportunity cost of time).
*   **Formula:** $$PS = \text{Price Received} - \text{Cost of Production}$$
*   **Graphical Representation:** The area **below the price** and **above the supply curve**.

### 2.3.3 Total Surplus and Efficiency
#### Economic Welfare
**Total Surplus (TS)** is the sum of Consumer Surplus and Producer Surplus.
*   **Formula:** $$TS = CS + PS = \text{Value to Buyers} - \text{Cost to Sellers}$$
*   **Efficiency:** An allocation is efficient if it maximizes Total Surplus.
*   **The Social Planner:** A benevolent planner cannot improve upon the market equilibrium outcome because the equilibrium already:
    1.  Allocates goods to buyers who value them most.
    2.  Allocates demand to sellers who produce at the lowest cost.
    3.  Realizes all potential gains from trade.

---

## 2.4 Elasticity
### 2.4.1 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)
#### Definition
A quantitative measure of how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price.
*   **Formula (Standard):** $$PED = \frac{\% \text{ Change in Quantity Demanded}}{\% \text{ Change in Price}}$$
*   **Interpretation:** We use the **absolute value** for easier comparison.

#### Elasticity Categories
*   **Elastic ($PED > 1$):** Quantity is very sensitive to price changes.
*   **Inelastic ($PED < 1$):** Quantity is not very sensitive.
*   **Unitary Elastic ($PED = 1$):** Quantity changes proportionately with price.
*   **Perfectly Inelastic ($PED = 0$):** Vertical demand curve; quantity never changes.
*   **Perfectly Elastic ($PED = \infty$):** Horizontal demand curve; any price change drops quantity to zero.

### 2.4.2 Elasticity on a Linear Demand Curve
Along a linear inverse demand curve ($P = A - BQ$):
*   **Formula:** $$PED = \frac{1}{B} \times \frac{P}{Q}$$
*   Elasticity **decreases** as we move down the curve (as $Q$ increases).
*   **Midpoint ($Q = A/2B$):** Demand is unitary elastic. Above this point, it is elastic; below this point, it is inelastic.

### 2.4.3 Determinants of Price Elasticity
| Factor | More Elastic if... | More Inelastic if... |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Substitutes** | Many close substitutes available (e.g., Crocin). | No close substitutes (e.g., patented medicine). |
| **Necessity vs. Luxury** | Good is a luxury (e.g., Mountain Dew). | Good is a necessity (e.g., Rice, Cigarettes). |
| **Market Definition** | Narrowly defined (e.g., Samsung Galaxy). | Broadly defined (e.g., all smartphones). |
| **Budget Share** | High portion of income (e.g., a car). | Low portion of income (e.g., stapler pins). |
| **Time Horizon** | Long run (consumers can adjust behavior). | Short run. |

### 2.4.4 Elasticity and Total Revenue
Total Revenue ($TR$) is $P \times Q$.
*   **Inelastic Demand:** Price and Revenue move in the **same** direction. (Price $\uparrow \rightarrow TR \uparrow$)
*   **Elastic Demand:** Price and Revenue move in **opposite** directions. (Price $\uparrow \rightarrow TR \downarrow$)
*   **Unitary Elastic:** Price changes do not affect Total Revenue.

### 2.4.5 Other Elasticities
*   **Income Elasticity ($E_I$):** $\frac{\% \Delta QD}{\% \Delta \text{Income}}$.
    *   Positive ($> 0$): **Normal Good**.
    *   Negative ($< 0$): **Inferior Good**.
*   **Cross-Price Elasticity ($E_{XY}$):** $\frac{\% \Delta QD_X}{\% \Delta P_Y}$.
    *   Positive ($> 0$): **Substitutes**.
    *   Negative ($< 0$): **Complements**.
*   **Price Elasticity of Supply ($E_S$):** $\frac{\% \Delta QS}{\% \Delta P}$. Depends on production flexibility and time.

---

## 2.5 Government Intervention
### 2.5.1 Tax Incidence
#### The Tax Wedge
When a specific tax ($T$) is imposed, it creates a wedge where $P_{Buyer} - P_{Seller} = T$.
*   **Burden Distribution:** The burden of a tax falls more heavily on the side of the market that is **more inelastic**.
*   **Luxury Tax Example:** Luxury goods (Yachts) have elastic demand but inelastic supply (specialized labor). Thus, sellers bear most of the tax burden.

#### Deadweight Loss (DWL)
*   Taxes reduce the quantity sold from $Q^*$ to $\hat{Q}$.
*   **DWL** is the fall in Total Surplus that results from a market distortion (like a tax).
*   **Formula (Linear curves):** $$DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{T^2}{B + D}$$
*   The more **elastic** the supply or demand, the **larger** the deadweight loss.

### 2.5.2 Price Controls
#### Price Ceiling (Price Cap)
A legal maximum on the price at which a good can be sold.
*   **Effective (Binding):** If the ceiling is **below** the equilibrium price ($P_C < P^*$).
*   **Outcome:** Causes **Excess Demand (Shortage)** and rationing issues.

#### Price Floor
A legal minimum on the price at which a good can be sold.
*   **Effective (Binding):** If the floor is **above** the equilibrium price ($P_F > P^*$).
*   **Outcome:** Causes **Excess Supply (Surplus)**. Used to safeguard producers.

---

## Quick Revision Summary
*   **Market Equilibrium:** $QD = QS$. Markets clear at $P^*$.
*   **Surplus:** $CS = WTP - P$; $PS = P - \text{Cost}$. Efficiency maximizes $TS$.
*   **PED Formula:** $\frac{\% \Delta QD}{\% \Delta P}$. Steep curve = Inelastic; Flat curve = Elastic.
*   **Revenue Rule:** If demand is elastic, lower the price to increase revenue. If inelastic, raise it.
*   **Income Elasticity:** Positive for normal goods; Negative for inferior.
*   **Cross-Price Elasticity:** Positive for substitutes; Negative for complements.
*   **Tax Burden:** Always falls on the more **inelastic** side.
*   **Binding Controls:** Ceiling must be *below* $P^*$; Floor must be *above* $P^*$.

# Module 3: Behaviour of a Firm in a Perfectly Competitive Market

## 3.1 Welcome to the Module
### Objectives of the Firm
*   **Primary Goal:** The fundamental assumption in microeconomics is that the goal of a firm is to **maximize profits**.
*   **Profit Definition:** Profit is the difference between Total Revenue and Total Cost.
    *   $$\text{Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Total Cost}$$
*   **Sustainability:** Regardless of whether a firm is private, family-owned, or non-profit, it must eventually earn positive profits to remain financially self-reliant and sustainable.

---

## 3.2 Behaviour of a Firm
### Revenue and Cost Components
#### Total Revenue ($TR$)
The amount a firm receives for the sale of its output.
*   **Calculation:** Price ($P$) $\times$ Quantity ($Q$).
*   **Average Revenue ($AR$):** Total revenue divided by the quantity sold ($TR/Q$). In perfect competition, $AR = P$.
*   **Marginal Revenue ($MR$):** The change in total revenue from selling one additional unit of output. In perfect competition, $MR = P$.

#### Total Cost ($TC$)
The market value of the inputs used in production. This includes both **Explicit** and **Implicit** costs.

| Cost Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Explicit Costs** | Costs that require a direct monetary outlay. | Wages, rent, raw materials. |
| **Implicit Costs** | Costs that do not require a cash outlay (Opportunity Costs). | Foregone wages from another job, interest on invested capital. |

### Economic vs. Accounting Profit
*   **Accounting Profit:** $Total Revenue - Total Explicit Costs$.
*   **Economic Profit:** $Total Revenue - (Explicit Costs + Implicit Costs)$.
*   **Key Insight:** Economic profit is always smaller than accounting profit because it accounts for the value of the next best alternative. Firms make decisions based on **Economic Profit**.

---

## 3.3 Cost Categories
### Fixed, Variable, and Sunk Costs
*   **Fixed Costs ($FC$):** Costs that do not vary with the quantity of output produced. They are incurred even if output is zero (e.g., rent, insurance).
*   **Variable Costs ($VC$):** Costs that change as the firm alters the quantity of output (e.g., raw materials, hourly labor).
*   **Sunk Costs:** Costs that have already been committed and cannot be recovered. 
    *   **Decision Rule:** Sunk costs should be **ignored** when making production decisions. They do not affect profit-maximizing choices at the margin.

### The Time Horizon
*   **Short Run:** A time period where at least one input (like factory size or machinery) is fixed.
*   **Long Run:** A period long enough for all inputs to become variable. Firms can build new factories or exit the industry entirely.

---

## 3.4 Marginal Principle
### The Production Function
The relationship between the quantity of inputs used and the quantity of output produced.

#### Marginal Product ($MP$)
The increase in output obtained from one additional unit of input.
*   $$MP = \frac{\Delta \text{Quantity}}{\Delta \text{Input}}$$
*   **Diminishing Marginal Product:** A property whereby the marginal product of an input declines as the quantity of the input increases (e.g., too many workers in a fixed-size kitchen leading to crowding).

---

## 3.5 Revenue and Cost of a Firm
### Short-Run Cost Curves
1.  **Average Fixed Cost ($AFC$):** $FC / Q$. It always declines as output increases because the fixed cost is spread over more units.
2.  **Average Variable Cost ($AVC$):** $VC / Q$. Typically U-shaped.
3.  **Average Total Cost ($ATC$):** $TC / Q$ or $AFC + AVC$. Also U-shaped. 
    *   **Efficient Scale:** The quantity of output that minimizes $ATC$.
4.  **Marginal Cost ($MC$):** The change in total cost from producing one additional unit ($\Delta TC / \Delta Q$).

#### Relationship Between Curves
*   When $MC < ATC$, Average Total Cost is falling.
*   When $MC > ATC$, Average Total Cost is rising.
*   **Intersection:** The $MC$ curve crosses the $ATC$ and $AVC$ curves at their **minimum** points.

### Profit Maximization in Perfect Competition
#### Characteristics of Perfectly Competitive Markets
*   Many buyers and many sellers.
*   Goods offered are largely the same (homogeneous).
*   Firms are **Price Takers** (cannot influence the market price).

#### The Profit-Maximizing Rule
A firm maximizes profit by producing the quantity where **Price equals Marginal Cost**:
$$P = MR = MC$$
*   If $P > MC$, the firm should increase production.
*   If $P < MC$, the firm should decrease production.

### Shutdown vs. Exit Decisions
*   **Temporary Shutdown (Short Run):** A firm stops production but still pays fixed costs.
    *   **Rule:** Shut down if $P < \text{min } AVC$.
*   **Exit (Long Run):** A firm leaves the industry permanently and pays zero costs.
    *   **Rule:** Exit if $P < \text{min } ATC$.
*   **Entry Rule:** Enter the industry if $P > \text{min } ATC$ (potential for positive economic profit).

---

## 3.6 Practice Problem
### Numerical Analysis
**Given:** $TC = 50 + 0.5Q^2$ and $MC = Q$. Market Demand $Q = 120 - P$.
*   **Fixed Cost:** 50.
*   **Variable Cost:** $0.5Q^2$.
*   **Average Total Cost ($ATC$):** $(50/Q) + 0.5Q$.
*   **Find Minimum $ATC$:** Set $MC = ATC \Rightarrow Q = (50/Q) + 0.5Q \Rightarrow 0.5Q = 50/Q \Rightarrow Q^2 = 100 \Rightarrow Q = 10$.
*   **Short-Run Equilibrium (9 firms):** $MC = P \Rightarrow Q = P$. Total Supply $Qs = 9P$. Set $Qs = Qd \Rightarrow 9P = 120 - P \Rightarrow 10P = 120 \Rightarrow P = 12$.
*   **Long-Run Equilibrium:** Occurs where $P = \text{min } ATC$. Since min $ATC = 10$ at $Q = 10$, the long-run price $P = 10$.

---

## Quick Revision Summary
*   **Profit Max Rule:** $P = MC$.
*   **Shutdown Rule (Short Run):** $P < AVC$.
*   **Exit Rule (Long Run):** $P < ATC$.
*   **Marginal Product:** Declines due to diminishing returns.
*   **Efficient Scale:** The point where $ATC$ is at its minimum.
*   **Sunk Costs:** Already paid and unrecoverable; ignore them in marginal decisions.
*   **Economic Profit:** Includes opportunity costs; zero economic profit means the firm is "breaking even" but covering all opportunity costs.

# Module 4: Monopoly

## 4.1 Welcome to the Module
### 4.1.1 Definition of Monopoly
#### Single Seller
A **Monopoly** is a market structure characterized by a single seller of a product for which there are no close substitutes. 

#### Market Power
Unlike firms in a perfectly competitive market, a monopolist is a **Price Maker**. This means the firm has the power to influence the market price of the good it sells.

---

## 4.2 Monopoly
### 4.2.1 Causes of Monopolies (Barriers to Entry)
Monopolies exist because other firms cannot enter the market and compete. These barriers to entry arise from three main sources:

1.  **Government Regulation:** 
    *   **Patents:** Exclusive rights granted by the government to an inventor for a specific period (e.g., AbbVie's 19-year patent for the drug Humira). Patents provide incentives for research and innovation.
    *   **Copyrights:** Exclusive rights for authors and software creators (e.g., Microsoft Windows, Wolfram Mathematica).
    *   **State-Created Monopolies:** Industries deemed of national importance (e.g., Indian Railways, HAL, and DRDO).
2.  **Control of Scarce Resources:** 
    *   A single firm owns a key resource required for production (e.g., De Beers Diamond Consortium, which once controlled 80-85% of the diamond market).
3.  **Natural Monopolies:** 
    *   An industry is a **Natural Monopoly** when a single firm can supply a good or service to an entire market at a lower cost than could two or more firms.
    *   **Cost Structure:** Characterized by huge **Fixed Costs** and negligible **Marginal Costs**.
    *   **Average Total Cost (ATC):** The ATC curve is continually decreasing as output increases. An incumbent producing large quantities ($Q_1$) at low cost ($P_1$) makes it impossible for small entrants ($Q_2$) to survive as their costs ($P_2$) would be higher.

### 4.2.2 Monopoly vs. Perfect Competition
| Feature | Perfectly Competitive Firm | Monopolist |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Control over Price** | Price Taker (No control) | Price Maker (Full control) |
| **Demand Curve** | Horizontal (Perfectly Elastic) | Downward-sloping (Market Demand) |
| **Marginal Revenue** | $MR = P$ | $MR < P$ |
| **Quantity Choice** | Can sell any amount at market price | Must lower price to sell more |

### 4.2.3 Profit Maximization
#### The Marginal Principle
A monopolist maximizes profit by choosing the quantity where **Marginal Revenue (MR) equals Marginal Cost (MC)**.
*   If $MR > MC$: The firm should increase production to raise profit.
*   If $MR < MC$: The firm should decrease production to raise profit.

#### Relationship between Demand and Marginal Revenue
If the inverse demand curve is linear:
$$P = A - BQ$$
Then the **Marginal Revenue** curve is:
$$MR = A - 2BQ$$
*Note: The MR curve starts at the same point on the vertical axis as the demand curve but has twice the slope.*

#### The Two-Step Decision Process
1.  **Quantity:** Find $Q_M$ where $MR = MC$.
2.  **Price:** Use the **Demand Curve** to find the highest price ($P_M$) consumers are willing to pay for that quantity $Q_M$.
*   **Condition:** The monopolist will only operate if profits are non-negative ($P \ge ATC$). High fixed costs can lead to losses even at the $MR=MC$ point.

---

## 4.3 Social Cost of Monopoly
### 4.3.1 Welfare Analysis
Monopolies fail to allocate resources efficiently compared to competitive markets.

1.  **Price and Quantity:**
    *   $P_{Monopoly} > P_{Competitive}$
    *   $Q_{Monopoly} < Q_{Competitive}$
2.  **Surplus Transfer:** 
    *   A portion of the **Consumer Surplus** is transferred to the monopolist as profit.
3.  **Deadweight Loss (DWL):**
    *   Monopoly creates a "wedge" between the consumers' willingness to pay and the producers' costs. 
    *   Since the monopolist produces less than the socially efficient quantity, some mutually beneficial trades do not take place.
    *   **Formula for DWL (Linear):** $$\frac{1}{2} \times (P_M - MC) \times (Q^* - Q_M)$$
4.  **Reduced Innovation:** Lack of competition may lead to slower technological progress over time.

---

## 4.4 Price Discrimination
### 4.4.1 Fundamentals of Price Discrimination
**Price Discrimination** is the business practice of selling the same good at different prices to different customers.

*   **Basis:** It is based on the consumer's **Willingness to Pay (WTP)**.
*   **Targeting:** Customers with higher WTP or higher intensity of demand are charged higher prices.

### 4.4.2 Requirements and Barriers
To practice price discrimination successfully, two conditions must be met:
1.  **Market Power:** The firm must be a monopolist or have significant control.
2.  **Prevention of Arbitrage:** The firm must prevent **Arbitrage** (the process of buying a good at a low price in one market and reselling it at a higher price in another).

#### Barriers to Arbitrage
*   **Social Norms/Marketing:** Creating a perception that products are different (e.g., "Pink Tax" on women's products like razors or medicine vs. men's products).
*   **Damaged Goods:** Intentionally reducing the quality of a product to cater to low-WTP segments (e.g., IBM limiting printer speed via a chip).

## 4.1 Welcome to the Module
### 4.1.1 Definition of Monopoly
#### Single Seller
A **Monopoly** is a market structure characterized by a single seller of a product for which there are no close substitutes. 

#### Market Power
Unlike firms in a perfectly competitive market, a monopolist is a **Price Maker**. This means the firm has the power to influence the market price of the good it sells.

---

## 4.2 Monopoly
### 4.2.1 Causes of Monopolies (Barriers to Entry)
Monopolies exist because other firms cannot enter the market and compete. These barriers to entry arise from three main sources:

1.  **Government Regulation:** 
    *   **Patents:** Exclusive rights granted by the government to an inventor for a specific period (e.g., AbbVie's 19-year patent for the drug Humira). Patents provide incentives for research and innovation.
    *   **Copyrights:** Exclusive rights for authors and software creators (e.g., Microsoft Windows, Wolfram Mathematica).
    *   **State-Created Monopolies:** Industries deemed of national importance (e.g., Indian Railways, HAL, and DRDO).
2.  **Control of Scarce Resources:** 
    *   A single firm owns a key resource required for production (e.g., De Beers Diamond Consortium, which once controlled 80-85% of the diamond market).
3.  **Natural Monopolies:** 
    *   An industry is a **Natural Monopoly** when a single firm can supply a good or service to an entire market at a lower cost than could two or more firms.
    *   **Cost Structure:** Characterized by huge **Fixed Costs** and negligible **Marginal Costs**.
    *   **Average Total Cost (ATC):** The ATC curve is continually decreasing as output increases. An incumbent producing large quantities ($Q_1$) at low cost ($P_1$) makes it impossible for small entrants ($Q_2$) to survive as their costs ($P_2$) would be higher.

### 4.2.2 Monopoly vs. Perfect Competition
| Feature | Perfectly Competitive Firm | Monopolist |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Control over Price** | Price Taker (No control) | Price Maker (Full control) |
| **Demand Curve** | Horizontal (Perfectly Elastic) | Downward-sloping (Market Demand) |
| **Marginal Revenue** | $MR = P$ | $MR < P$ |
| **Quantity Choice** | Can sell any amount at market price | Must lower price to sell more |

### 4.2.3 Profit Maximization
#### The Marginal Principle
A monopolist maximizes profit by choosing the quantity where **Marginal Revenue (MR) equals Marginal Cost (MC)**.
*   If $MR > MC$: The firm should increase production to raise profit.
*   If $MR < MC$: The firm should decrease production to raise profit.

#### Relationship between Demand and Marginal Revenue
If the inverse demand curve is linear:
$$P = A - BQ$$
Then the **Marginal Revenue** curve is:
$$MR = A - 2BQ$$
*Note: The MR curve starts at the same point on the vertical axis as the demand curve but has twice the slope.*

#### The Two-Step Decision Process
1.  **Quantity:** Find $Q_M$ where $MR = MC$.
2.  **Price:** Use the **Demand Curve** to find the highest price ($P_M$) consumers are willing to pay for that quantity $Q_M$.
*   **Condition:** The monopolist will only operate if profits are non-negative ($P \ge ATC$). High fixed costs can lead to losses even at the $MR=MC$ point.

## 4.3 Social Cost of Monopoly
### 4.3.1 Welfare Analysis
Monopolies fail to allocate resources efficiently compared to competitive markets.

1.  **Price and Quantity:**
    *   $P_{Monopoly} > P_{Competitive}$
    *   $Q_{Monopoly} < Q_{Competitive}$
2.  **Surplus Transfer:** 
    *   A portion of the **Consumer Surplus** is transferred to the monopolist as profit.
3.  **Deadweight Loss (DWL):**
    *   Monopoly creates a "wedge" between the consumers' willingness to pay and the producers' costs. 
    *   Since the monopolist produces less than the socially efficient quantity, some mutually beneficial trades do not take place.
    *   **Formula for DWL (Linear):** $$\frac{1}{2} \times (P_M - MC) \times (Q^* - Q_M)$$
4.  **Reduced Innovation:** Lack of competition may lead to slower technological progress over time.

## 4.4 Price Discrimination
### 4.4.1 Fundamentals of Price Discrimination
**Price Discrimination** is the business practice of selling the same good at different prices to different customers.

*   **Basis:** It is based on the consumer's **Willingness to Pay (WTP)**.
*   **Targeting:** Customers with higher WTP or higher intensity of demand are charged higher prices.

### 4.4.2 Requirements and Barriers
To practice price discrimination successfully, two conditions must be met:
1.  **Market Power:** The firm must be a monopolist or have significant control.
2.  **Prevention of Arbitrage:** The firm must prevent **Arbitrage** (the process of buying a good at a low price in one market and reselling it at a higher price in another).

#### Barriers to Arbitrage
*   **Social Norms/Marketing:** Creating a perception that products are different (e.g., "Pink Tax" on women's products like razors or medicine vs. men's products).
*   **Damaged Goods:** Intentionally reducing the quality of a product to cater to low-WTP segments (e.g., IBM limiting printer speed via a chip).
*   **Screening:** Requiring identification (e.g., Student IDs for software discounts).

### 4.4.3 Examples of Price Discrimination
*   **Monuments:** Different entry fees for foreign nationals vs. local citizens.
*   **Purchase History:** Discounts for first-time buyers to attract "experimental" customers.
*   **Firm Size:** Software companies (like Oracle) charging higher prices to large enterprises than to small firms.
*   **Quantity Discounts:** Charging less per unit as the quantity purchased increases.
*   **Two-Part Tariffs:** A fixed fee to enter (e.g., amusement park) plus a variable fee per unit of usage (e.g., per ride).
